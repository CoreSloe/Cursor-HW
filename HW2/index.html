The History Of Street Art

Street art has a fascinating history. There are times that it has been illegal and cities have worked hard to clean it up, and there are times that it has been commissioned and cities have paid massive sums of money to have more work added to their streets. So, how could this form of visual art exist on two completely different sides of the legal and social spectrum? We are going to take a deep dive into the history of street art to answer that exact question. Let’s start with the absolute basics before we weave through the colorful past of graffiti and street art.

What is Street Art?

Put simply, street art is a form of artwork that is located in public spaces. The most common place to find street art is on walls like the sides of buildings, billboards, and other flat surfaces that can be easily viewed. In addition to walls, artists utilize sidewalks, windows, and even subway cars. Street art is also known as guerrilla art, graffiti, urban art, and independent art. It can be used to advertise businesses, spread socio-political messages, or just bring joy and color to different spaces. It’s expanded beyond just public places now. Famous street artists like Keith Haring, Shepard Fairey, and Banksy are extremely mainstream, household names, and their work is seen on t-shirts and even presidential campaign posters.Traditional street art, like the graffiti you’d recognize from the Fresh Prince of Bel-Air logo or the side of bus stops, is done with paint in aerosol spray cans. Some large pieces require additional tools like scaffolding or a cherry picker, but most of them are simple and done with just one guy and his or her paint. In recent years, the medium has been growing. You will find that some artists use new techniques like poster-sized stickers, mosaics made with different materials, and even video projections. There aren’t rules on what can or cannot be used, which is part of what makes street art so special. It’s created for the public to enjoy outside in open spaces instead of inside gallery walls requiring tickets for admission.
